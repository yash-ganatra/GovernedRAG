## Overview

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0) provides a structured, voluntary framework for organisations to manage risks associated with AI systems throughout their lifecycle. Published in January 2023, the framework consists of two parts: a conceptual framing of AI risk (Part 1) and the Core Framework with four functions: GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, and MANAGE (Part 2). This document focuses on the **MEASURE** and **GOVERN** functions, which are most directly relevant to AI governance and compliance.

---

## Framework Structure

The AI RMF Core is organised around four interdependent functions:

| Function | Abbreviation | Purpose |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| Govern | GV | Culture, policies, and accountability structures |
| Map | MP | Context setting and risk identification |
| Measure | MS | Analysis, assessment, and tracking of risks |
| Manage | MG | Prioritisation and treatment of risks |

Each function contains **Categories** and **Subcategories** that describe specific outcomes.

---

## GOVERN Function (GV)

### Purpose

The GOVERN function cultivates and implements organisational structures, policies, processes, and a culture of **AI risk management accountability** across the full AI lifecycle. It is the foundational function upon which the other three depend.

### GV-1: Policies, Processes, Procedures, and Practices

**GV-1.1** — AI risk management is framed, assessed, and tracked as part of organisational risk management processes.

**GV-1.2** — The risk tolerance of the organisation with respect to AI risk is established, communicated, and maintained.

**GV-1.3** — Organisational risk tolerance for AI is determined and communicated in a way that reflects the organisation's commitments to stakeholders.

**GV-1.4** — Organisational teams document the AI risks and benefits of their AI systems and communicate them to senior leadership.

**GV-1.5** — Organisational risk priorities related to AI are reflected in staffing and resource allocation decisions.

**GV-1.6** — Policies and procedures documenting the organisation's approach to governance and risk management are established, communicated, and enforced.

**GV-1.7** — Processes and procedures are in place for decommissioning and phasing out AI systems safely and in a manner that does not increase risks or decrease the organisation's trustworthiness.

### GV-2: Accountability Structures

**GV-2.1** — Roles and responsibilities — and their associated competencies — for personnel involved in AI risk management are documented, informed by relevant standards and guidelines, and maintained.

**GV-2.2** — The organisation's personnel and partners are provided AI risk management training to enable them to perform their duties and responsibilities consistent with related policies, procedures, norms and organisational risk tolerance.

**GV-2.3** — Executive leadership of the organisation takes responsibility for decisions about risks associated with AI system development, deployment, and operation.

### GV-3: Organisational Teams

**GV-3.1** — Organisational teams document the AI risks and benefits of their AI systems and communicate them to senior leadership.

**GV-3.2** — Senior leadership takes responsibility for decisions about risks associated with AI system development, deployment, and operation.

**GV-4: Organisational Teams are Committed to a Culture that Minimises Potential Negative Impacts**

**GV-4.1** — Organisational policies and practices are in place to foster a critical thinking and safety-first mindset in the design, development, deployment, and uses of AI systems to minimise potential negative impacts.

**GV-4.2** — Organisational teams document concerns about AI risks and share them with accountability leads.

**GV-5: Organisational Risk Policies**

**GV-5.1** — Organisational policies and practices intended to foster a critical thinking and safety-first mindset in the design, development, deployment, and uses of AI systems to minimise potential negative impacts are in place, communicated, and enforced.

**GV-6: Policies and Procedures**

**GV-6.1** — Policies and procedures that support AI risk management are established and maintained, including policies and procedures for risk identification, incident response, and continuous improvement.

**GV-6.2** — Contingency processes are in place for harmful AI system outputs, including policies and procedures for how decisions will be made to pause, adjust or shut down AI systems, and how those decisions will be implemented.

## MEASURE Function (MS)

### Purpose

The MEASURE function employs quantitative, qualitative, or mixed methods to **analyse, assess, benchmark, and monitor AI risk** and its impacts. It operationalises risk management by making risks visible and trackable.

### MS-1: AI Risk Analysis

**MS-1.1** — Approaches and metrics for measuring and monitoring AI risks, impacts, and effects are selected for implementation starting with the concept and design phases and continuing throughout the AI lifecycle.

**MS-1.2** — Appropriateness of AI metrics and effectiveness of existing controls are regularly assessed and updated, including reports of errors and incidents, in AI audit reports, feedback, and complaints received from consumers or other external sources.

**MS-1.3** — Internal experts who did not serve as front-line developers for the system and/or independent assessors are involved in regular assessments and updates. Their findings are easily accessible to relevant AI actors across the organisation.

### MS-2: AI Systems Testing

**MS-2.1** — Test sets, metrics, and details are documented.

**MS-2.2** — Scientific (or scientific evidence-based) methods and computing best practices are used to develop and test AI systems, including applying adversarial testing techniques such as red-teaming to identify and mitigate failure modes, hidden functionality, or emergent behaviours.

**MS-2.3** — AI system performance or assurance criteria are measured qualitatively or quantitatively and demonstrated for conditions similar to deployment setting(s). Measures are documented.

**MS-2.4** — The functionality and behaviour of the AI system and it component are monitored when in production.

**MS-2.5** — The AI system to be deployed satisfies its design goals and meets applicable requirements and industry best practices for performance and effectiveness across demographic groups.

**MS-2.6** — The risk or limitation of the AI system in deployment context(s) is communicated to deployers and users. Effective communication includes risk and limitation information relevant to the context of use.

**MS-2.7** — AI risk management is informed by empirically established or theoretical best practices, and applicable laws and regulations, including privacy-related laws.

**MS-2.8** — Risks associated with transparency and accountability — such as providing explanations that are not meaningful, outdated, or incorrect — are examined and documented.

**MS-2.9** — The AI system's outputs are regularly evaluated for accuracy, fairness, and alignment with the intended purpose.

**MS-2.10** — Privacy risk of the AI system — as identified in the MAP function — is examined and documented.

**MS-2.11** — Fairness and bias — as identified in the MAP function — are evaluated on a regular basis, using established methods.

**MS-2.12** — Environmental impact and sustainability of AI model training and operations are measured and documented.

### MS-3: AI System Evaluation

**MS-3.1** — Ongoing monitoring plans are in place, implemented, and documented for measuring and managing identified AI risks at the component- and system-level.

**MS-3.2** — Risk tracking approaches are considered for settings where AI risks are difficult to assess using currently available measurement techniques, or where metrics are not yet available.

**MS-3.3** — Feedback processes for end users and impacted communities to report problems and appeal system outcomes are established and integrated into AI system evaluation metrics.

### MS-4: AI Risk Measurement

**MS-4.1** — Measurement approaches for identifying AI risks are connected to deployment context(s) and informed by risk analysis results.

**MS-4.2** — Measurement results inform the prioritisation of AI risk treatment activities.

## AI Trustworthiness Characteristics (AI RMF)

The AI RMF identifies the following properties of trustworthy AI systems:

| Characteristic | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| **Accountable and Transparent** | AI actors are responsible; system is explainable |
| **Explainable and Interpretable** | Decisions can be understood by intended users |
| **Fair with Harmful Bias Managed** | Equitable treatment across demographic groups |
| **Privacy-Enhanced** | Data protection embedded by design |
| **Reliable** | Accurate and performs as intended |
| **Resilient** | Withstands attacks and unexpected inputs |
| **Safe** | Avoids harm to people and the environment |
| **Secure and Resilient** | Defends against adversarial threats |
| **Valid and Verified** | Meets specifications under intended conditions |

## Application to AI System Deployment

Organisations adopting the AI RMF should implement the GOVERN and MEASURE functions through:

1. **Establishing an AI Risk Register** — documenting all AI systems, their risks, and mitigations
2. **Conducting pre-deployment TEVV** — Testing, Evaluation, Verification, and Validation
3. **Implementing continuous monitoring** — dashboards tracking accuracy, fairness, drift, and anomalies
4. **Creating AI incident response plans** — procedures for detecting, containing, and learning from AI failures
5. **Documenting AI system cards** — model cards and data sheets for all deployed models
6. **Engaging affected communities** — feedback loops and appeal mechanisms for impacted individuals
